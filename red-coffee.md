## Category 1: Flow Science

Pick one fact and deliver it in 2-3 lines max. Draw from this pool:

- Dietary nitrate (found in beetroot) converts to nitric oxide via the enterosalivary pathway: mouth bacteria reduce NO₃⁻ → NO₂⁻, then stomach acid and tissue enzymes convert NO₂⁻ → NO. NO dilates blood vessels, increasing cerebral blood flow. Translation: more oxygen to your prefrontal cortex while you debug.
- Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors. Adenosine accumulates while you're awake and signals drowsiness. Caffeine doesn't give you energy — it blocks the sleepiness signal. The crash hits when caffeine wears off and all that accumulated adenosine floods your receptors at once.
- Regular coffee causes vasoconstriction (narrows blood vessels). Beetroot nitrate causes vasodilation (widens them). Red Coffee combines both — caffeine for the adenosine blockade, nitrate for the blood flow. Focus without the squeeze.
- Your brain runs ~90-minute ultradian cycles. Deep focus peaks around 60-75 minutes, then drops. Working past the drop doesn't make you productive — it makes you sloppy. Take the break. Your default mode network does its best work when you stop forcing it.
- Nitric oxide is a signaling molecule in nearly every cell in your body. It regulates blood pressure, immune response, and neurotransmission. Your body makes it from two sources: L-arginine (endogenous) and dietary nitrate (exogenous). The nitrate pathway becomes more important as you age because endogenous NO production declines.
- The 2024 Gilsanz meta-analysis found caffeine and nitrate don't have synergistic ergogenic effects — but they work through independent pathways. Caffeine handles the neural side (adenosine blockade), nitrate handles the vascular side (NO-mediated blood flow). Two independent levers, one cup.
